HIPAA EXPLAINED

The US law that protects health information — three rules, three families of safeguards, and one linchpin: the risk analysis (a 2025 update is proposed, not yet in force — see §09)

01
What it is

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act — the US federal law that protects health information. It sets rules for how Protected Health Information (PHI) may be used, disclosed, and safeguarded. Enforced by the HHS Office for Civil Rights (OCR).

Not just a privacy promise — a security programme you must build and prove.

02
Who it covers
🏥
Covered entities — healthcare providers, health plans, and clearinghouses.
🔗
Business associates — vendors that handle PHI on their behalf (cloud, billing, IT) — directly liable too.

A signed Business Associate Agreement (BAA) is required before a vendor touches PHI.

03
The three rules — privacy, security, and what to do after a breach

📋 Privacy Rule

Governs permitted uses & disclosures of PHI — in any form (paper, oral, electronic). Gives patients rights over their records.

🛡️ Security Rule

Safeguards for electronic PHI (ePHI) only. Administrative, physical & technical controls — 45 CFR Part 164.

📣 Breach Notification

Notify affected individuals + HHS within 60 days of discovery. 500+ in a state → notify prominent media too.

Privacy governs the “may I?”; Security protects the bytes; Breach Notification handles the aftermath.

04
Security Rule safeguards — three families guarding ePHI at the centre

Every safeguard is an implementation specification that is either Required or Addressable — and “addressable” does not mean optional (see §05).

05
Required vs addressable

Each spec carries one of two labels — and both create duties:

🔒
Required — implement it. No discretion.
⚖️
Addressable — implement it, or document why an equivalent alternative is reasonable.

“Addressable” ≠ optional — skipping it without justification is a violation.

06
Risk analysis · the linchpin

An accurate, thorough risk analysis of every place ePHI lives is the foundation the whole Security Rule stands on — you can't safeguard what you haven't mapped.

find the ePHI→ assess risk→ mitigate

The single most-cited failure in OCR enforcement actions.

07
Breach notification — the clock starts at discovery, not at the breach

60 days is the outer limit — “without unreasonable delay” means sooner where you can.

08
Enforcement · OCR

OCR sets tiered civil monetary penalties by culpability — the more you knew, the higher the band:

CulpabilityPenalty
Didn't knowlowest tier
Willful neglecthighest civil tier
Willful misusecriminal penalties

Willful misuse of PHI can bring fines and imprisonment.

09
Proposed 2025 update — NOT yet in force

Proposed only. OCR published an NPRM on 6 Jan 2025 (comments closed Mar 2025). As of mid-2026 it is not finalized. If adopted it would:

🔐
Make specs mandatory — remove the “addressable” distinction; require encryption of ePHI at rest & in transit + MFA.
🧭
New controls — network segmentation · asset inventories · vuln scans (≥ every 6 mo) · pen testing (≥ annually).

Treat as an incoming change — verify status against the current Federal Register before relying on it.

10
Watch out
🗺️
Skipped risk analysis — the number-one OCR finding; a checklist is not a risk analysis of where your ePHI actually lives.
❌
“Addressable = optional” myth — addressable still requires you to implement it or document a reasonable equivalent.
🔗
Forgotten business associates — vendors are directly liable; PHI needs a signed BAA before it changes hands.
11
The takeaway
HIPAA is three rules over PHI — and the Security Rule stands or falls on an honest risk analysis that maps every place your ePHI lives.
Map the ePHI→ Apply safeguards→ Sign BAAs→ Notify in 60 days
