01
What it is

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 (Feb 2024) — voluntary, sector- and country-agnostic guidance describing the outcomes an organization should achieve to manage cyber risk. Not a law, not a certification.

2.0's headline change — it now targets ALL organizations, not just critical infrastructure.

02
What changed in 2.0
🏛️
GOVERN added — a new sixth Function making cyber an enterprise-leadership issue.
🌐
Scope broadened — from critical infrastructure to every org, any size, any sector.
🧭
More help — Implementation Examples, Informative References & Quick-Start Guides.
03
The six Functions — GOVERN is the hub; it wraps the operational five

Govern is the connective tissue — leadership sets strategy, roles, policy, risk appetite & supply-chain expectations that steer Identify · Protect · Detect · Respond · Recover.

04
Why GOVERN was added — cyber becomes an enterprise-governance issue, owned by leadership
organizational context risk-management strategy roles & responsibilities policy oversight supply chain — C-SCRM

The point: cybersecurity is a leadership & risk decision, not just an IT task buried in the ops team.

05
Three parts — the Core, the Profiles, and the Tiers

🎯 The Core

Functions → Categories → Subcategories — a hierarchy of desired outcomes (not tasks).

🗺️ Profiles

Current vs Target state — the gap between them is your prioritized roadmap.

📶 Tiers

1 → 4 — how rigorous & adaptive your risk practices are, from Partial to Adaptive.

The Core says what good looks like; Profiles show where you are; Tiers describe how mature the practice is.

06
The four Tiers
TierRisk practice
1 · PartialAd-hoc, reactive
2 · Risk-InformedAware, uneven
3 · RepeatableFormal, org-wide
4 · AdaptiveContinuously improving

Tiers describe rigor — they're not a maturity grade you must "reach 4" on.

07
Outcomes → controls

CSF says what to achieve. You realize it by mapping each outcome to a concrete control set:

NIST SP 800-53CIS ControlsISO 27001

The framework is the map; the control catalog is the terrain you actually build on.

08
Using it — a Current Profile → Target Profile gap drives the plan

Assess Current, define Target from your risk appetite — the gap becomes a prioritized, budgeted plan.

09
CSF ≠ RMF — voluntary outcomes vs a mandatory federal process
🧭
CSF (this) — voluntary framework of outcomes; the "what to achieve", usable by anyone.
⚖️
RMF · SP 800-37 — the mandatory federal process to authorize a system to operate (ATO).

Different tools: CSF frames the goals; RMF is the step-by-step process federal systems must follow.

10
Watch out
📋
Reading it as a checklist — it's outcomes, not a pass/fail control list.
🏛️
Skipping Govern — the new Function is the point; without it the other five drift.
🧱
Framework ≠ controls — CSF doesn't implement anything; map it to 800-53 / CIS / ISO.
